# Stakeholders

## Who Cares About This Project

Building software isn't just about code. It's about people — the ones who use it, pay for it, build it, and keep it running.

## Primary Stakeholders

### End Users

The people actually clicking buttons and filling forms.

**Customers booking resources**

They just want to book something without hassle. If the app is confusing, slow, or unreliable, they'll go back to calling. Or use a competitor.

*What they care about:* Speed, simplicity, reliability, fair pricing.

**Resource managers**

These are our paying customers. They're running businesses. They adopted Ajasta to solve problems, not create new ones.

*What they care about:* Reduced workload, fewer no-shows, clear visibility into their bookings.

**Administrators**

The people managing the platform. They need to see what's happening, handle problems, keep things running.

*What they care about:* Stability, good tooling, clear documentation.

### Business Stakeholders

**Product owner / founder**

Sets direction, prioritizes features, makes the hard calls about what to build and what to postpone.

*What they care about:* User growth, revenue, product-market fit.

**Development team**

We're building this thing. Our lives are easier when requirements are clear, architecture makes sense, and we're not constantly fighting technical debt.

*What we care about:* Manageable scope, clean code, reasonable deadlines.

**Operations**

Deployment, monitoring, incident response. When something breaks at 2am, these folks are awake.

*What they care about:* Observability, automation, documentation.

## Secondary Stakeholders

### Integration Partners

**LinkSaver team**

Our sister project. The integration between booking and knowledge management is a key differentiator. We need to coordinate APIs and data models.

*What they care about:* Clean integration points, shared user management, coherent user experience.

**Stripe**

Payment processing. We're trusting them with money, and they with ours.

*What they care about:* Compliance, proper implementation, not getting fraud.

**AWS**

We store images on S3. Not the most critical dependency, but still.

### External Parties

**Regulatory bodies**

GDPR compliance. Data protection. Consumer rights. Not exciting, but necessary.

**Competitors**

They're watching us. We're watching them. The market decides who wins.

## Stakeholder Communication

Different groups need different information at different times.

| Group | What they need | How often |
|-------|----------------|-----------|
| Customers | Feature updates, bug fixes | As needed |
| Resource managers | New capabilities, billing | Monthly |
| Development team | Requirements, blockers | Daily/weekly |
| Operations | Alerts, capacity planning | Real-time |
| Product owner | Progress, metrics, blockers | Weekly |

## Success Means Different Things

For a customer: "I booked my court in 30 seconds and showed up to find it actually reserved."

For a salon owner: "My no-show rate dropped from 20% to 5% because people prepay."

For the dev team: "We shipped the feature without breaking anything and went home on time."

For the business: "Monthly recurring revenue is growing 15% month over month."

## Conflicts and Trade-offs

Sometimes stakeholders want different things.

**Speed vs. quality:** Customers want features now. Developers want to write good code. Operations wants stability.

**Flexibility vs. simplicity:** Resource managers want configurable rules. Customers want a simple interface. You can't maximize both.

**Privacy vs. analytics:** Users want their data protected. The business needs data to make decisions.

Finding the balance is the job.

## Keeping Everyone Happy (Or At Least Informed)

You can't please everyone all the time. But you can:

1. **Communicate early** — No surprises
2. **Explain the why** — People accept trade-offs when they understand the reasoning
3. **Deliver value** — If the core promise is met, minor friction is forgiven
4. **Be honest** — Admit mistakes, share bad news, don't overpromise
